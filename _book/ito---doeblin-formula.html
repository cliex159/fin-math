3 Ito - Doeblin Formula

3.1 Riemann Approach

Slide 79

Compute the Riemann integral \(\int_0^1x^2\,dx=\) by using definition

Let \(\Pi\) be a partition of \([0, 1]\): \[0 = t_0 < t_1 = \frac{1}{n} < t_2 = \frac{2}{n} < ... < t_i < ... < t_n = 1,t_i = \frac{i}{n}\]

Since \(f(x) = x^2\) is a continuous function of \([0, 1]\), the Riemann integral of \(f(x)\) on \([0, 1]\) is \[\begin{align*} \int_0^1x^2\,dx &= \lim_{n\to\infty} \sum_{i=0}^{n-1}(t_{i+1}-t_i)f(t_i) \\ &=\lim_{n\to\infty} \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} \frac{1}{n} \left( \frac{i }{n}\right)^2 \\ &=\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{n^3} \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} i^2 \\ &=\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{(n-1)(n)(2n-1)}{6n^3} \\ &=\frac{1}{3} \end{align*}\]

Answer: \(\int_0^1x^2\,dx=\frac{1}{3}\)

Compute the Riemann integral \(\int_0^2x^2\,dx=\) by using definition

Let \(\Pi\) be a partition of \([0, 2]\): \[0 = t_0 < t_1 = \frac{2}{n} < t_2 = \frac{4}{n} < ... < t_i < ... < t_n = 2,t_i = \frac{2i}{n}\]

Since \(f(x) = x^2\) is a continuous function of \([0, 2]\), the Riemann integral of \(f(x)\) on \([0, 2]\) is \[\begin{align*} \int_0^2x^2\,dx &= \lim_{n\to\infty} \sum_{i=0}^{n-1}(t_{i+1}-t_i)f(t_i) \\ &=\lim_{n\to\infty} \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} \frac{2}{n} \left( \frac{2i }{n}\right)^2 \\ &=\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{8}{n^3} \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} i^2 \\ &=\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{8(n-1)(n)(2n-1)}{6n^3} \\ &=\frac{8}{3} \end{align*}\]

Answer: \(\int_0^2x^2\,dx=\frac{8}{3}\)

3.4 Taylor Expansion

Slide 97

For one-variable functions, their second-order Taylor series expansions have the form
\[df(x)=f'(x)\,dx+\frac{1}{2}f''(x)\,dx^2\]

  • Find the second-order Taylor series expansion of \(f(x)=e^x\)

We have \(df(x) = f'(x)\,dx + \frac{1}{2}f''(x)\,dx^2\). Therefore, the second-order Taylor series will be
\[f(x) = f(x_0) + f'(x_0)(x-x_0) + \frac{1}{2}f''(x_0)(x-x_0)^2\]

Let \(f(x)=e^x\) and \(x_0=0\), thus
\[\begin{align*} f(x) &= e^0 + e^0x + \frac{1}{2}e^0(x)^2 \\ &= \frac{1}{2}x^2 + x + 1 \end{align*}\]

  • Find the second-order Taylor series expansion of \(f(x)=\sin x\)

We have \(df(x) = f'(x)\,dx + \frac{1}{2}f''(x)\,dx^2\). Therefore, the second-order Taylor series will be
\[f(x) = f(x_0) + f'(x_0)(x-x_0) + \frac{1}{2}f''(x_0)(x-x_0)^2\]

Let \(f(x)=\sin (x)\) and \(x_0=0\), thus
\[\begin{align*} f(x) &= \sin (0) + x \cos (0) - \frac{1}{2} \sin (x)^2 \\ &= x \end{align*}\]

  • Find the second-order Taylor series expansion of \(f(x)=\ln x,\,x>0\)

We have \(df(x) = f'(x)\,dx + \frac{1}{2}f''(x)\,dx^2\). Therefore, the second-order Taylor series will be
\[f(x) = f(x_0) + f'(x_0)(x-x_0) + \frac{1}{2}f''(x_0)(x-x_0)^2\]

Let \(f(x)=\ln x\) and \(x_0=1\), thus
\[\begin{align*} f(x) &= \ln (1) + x-1 - \frac{1}{2}(x-1)^2 \\ &= x-1 - \frac{1}{2}(x-1)^2 \\ &= -\frac{1}{2}x^2+2x-\frac{3}{2} \end{align*}\]

\[\begin{align*} (X_4|X_2=3) &\sim \log\mathcal{N} \left(\ln 3+\frac{584}{5},\frac{992}{5} \right) \\ \end{align*}\]

\[\begin{align*} E[X_4|X_2=3]&=e^{\ln 3+\frac{584}{5}+ \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{992}{5}} \\ &=e^{217.09} \\ Var(X_4|X_2=3)&=\left(e^{\frac{992}{5}}-1\right)e^{\left(2(\ln 3+\frac{584}{5})+\frac{992}{5} \right)} \\ &=(e^{198.4}-1)e^{434.19} \end{align*}\]

Answer: The mean and variance of \((X_4|X_2=3)\) are \(e^{217.09}\) and \((e^{198.4}-1)e^{434.19}\)
